What Is Actually Here

The floor is built around clear categories, not one generic gift mix.

The store still feels like one trading company, but the easiest way to read it is by the categories people actually come in for: beads, fabric and ribbon, jewelry, Pendleton and apparel, leatherwork, pottery, candles, soaps, foods, toys, puzzles, and the museum side of the stop.

Beads / Beading Kits

The store carries more than finished beadwork

Loose beads and kits give the store a real beading-supplies side instead of limiting everything to finished gift pieces in the case.

Fabric / Ribbon / Textiles

Pattern fabric keeps the materials side easy to spot

Fabric and ribbon keep the textile side of the store from collapsing into one blanket wall. They help show the broader materials-and-pattern story on the floor.

Jewelry

Case pieces still anchor the close-up browse

Jewelry is still one of the categories people most strongly associate with the store, especially when they want handmade work in a smaller format.

Pendleton / Apparel

Blankets, outerwear, and pattern-driven apparel

Pendleton blankets and apparel give the store its strongest textile-and-Western identity without turning the whole room into one brand story.

Leatherwork / Bags

Bags and carry pieces broaden the trading-post feel

Leatherwork and bags make the floor feel broader than a case-and-art shop. They sit naturally between apparel, gifts, and the rest of the browse.

Huckleberry / Candles / Toys

Foods and smaller shelf finds keep the store from feeling repetitive

Huckleberry foods, candles, soaps, toys, puzzles, pottery, and smaller shelf goods round out the broader mix, so the store never reads like only apparel or only jewelry.

Artifacts On Site

Museum artifacts are part of what gives the collections side extra depth.

The trading company does not stop at retail shelves. Museum artifact displays and handmade work on site help slow the visit down and give the room more depth.
