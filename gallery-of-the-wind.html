Gallery of the Wind & Museum

A quieter room inside a real Fort Washakie trading post.

The gallery gives Wind River Trading Company its deeper register: artwork, artifacts, and display pieces connected to Shoshone and Arapaho culture, set apart from the sales floor but part of the same visit.

Why It Matters

This is what makes the visit more than shopping.

You can come in for Pendleton, beadwork, or supplies and then step into a room that asks for a different pace.

That change of register is part of what gives Wind River Trading Company its own identity.

Quieter pace The gallery is set apart from the sales floor, so the work is not competing with the counters and racks.
Cultural focus The room centers artwork and artifacts connected to Shoshone and Arapaho culture.
Same visit The gallery is not offsite or detached from the store; it stays part of the trading-post experience.

What You Can Spend Time With

More than a token display at the edge of a gift shop.

The museum side includes handmade art, artifacts, and related display pieces that give the building breadth beyond the store floor.

In The Room

Artwork, artifacts, and display pieces that deserve their own room.

Visitors may see handmade art, painted pieces, and museum material set apart from the sales floor.

Artwork Handmade art and painted work help define the room.
Artifacts Artifacts and related display pieces hold the museum side together.
Related objects Rugs, drums, pottery, pipes, and other display work extend what the gallery covers.

See Both Sides

The trading post and gallery work best together.

One side shows what people still use and shop for. The other slows the visit down and gives it context. That pairing is what makes the building memorable.
