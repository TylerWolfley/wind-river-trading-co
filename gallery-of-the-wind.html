Inside Wind River Trading Company

A room of artwork, photographs, beadwork, and older pieces.

Gallery of the Wind is inside Wind River Trading Company in Fort Washakie. The room includes Eastern Shoshone and Northern Arapaho artwork, artifacts, photographs, beadwork, moccasins, tools, pipes, painted pieces, and display cases.

It does not feel like a formal gallery. It feels closer to a trading-post room: pieces gathered over time, set close enough that you have to slow down and look.

Where it is Inside Wind River Trading Company at 14631 Hwy. 287 in Fort Washakie.
What it holds Artwork, artifacts, photographs, beadwork, moccasins, tools, pipes, and display cases.
How to see it Give it a few minutes. The small pieces are easy to miss.

Display highlights

Chief Washakie, beadwork, old photographs, and handmade work.

The room mixes local history with the kind of handmade work and older objects that collect around a long-running trading post.

Chief Washakie

The Crowheart Butte story.

One wall display centers on Chief Washakie, the Eastern Shoshone leader whose name is carried by Fort Washakie. The panel presents the 1866 Crowheart Butte story, where Washakie and Crow leader Big Robber are remembered in a single-combat account tied to hunting grounds in the Wind River Range.

The display roots the room in Fort Washakie. It points to the people and stories around the store, not just decoration.

Beadwork

Handmade pieces deserve a closer look.

Moccasins, beadwork, and small handmade pieces are easy to pass by too quickly. They are part of the reason the room needs a slower walk-through.

The details matter here: bead colors, stitching, worn materials, and the way these pieces sit among older photographs and cases.

What to look for

What to look for in the room.

Look for beadwork, moccasins, handmade art, historic photographs, tools, pipes, pottery, painted pieces, and display cases that connect the gallery back to the store around it.

In the room

Eastern Shoshone and Northern Arapaho artwork, artifacts, and display pieces.

At its simplest, the gallery is a room of Shoshone and Arapaho artwork and artifacts. In person, it is also a trading-post display room, with older tools, photographs, handmade work, and case groupings.
